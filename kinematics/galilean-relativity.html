🎮 How to Use the Simulation

Step 1: Set the Train Velocity

Use the "Train velocity" slider to set how fast the train moves relative to the ground (0 – 20 m/s).

Step 2: Drop the Ball

Click "Drop the ball". The same event is shown simultaneously in two reference frames:

  • Left — S′ (Train Frame): The camera rides with the train. The blue passenger sits inside the wagon and sees the ball fall in a straight blue vertical line.
  • Right — S (Ground Frame): The camera is fixed to the ground. The whole wagon slides past. The blue figure (passenger inside the transparent wagon) shows his path as a blue dashed line — straight in his own frame. The green observer standing beside the track sees the same ball trace a green parabola.

Color coding

  • ■ Blue — the passenger's perspective: ball, figure, and straight drop (train frame S′)
  • ■ Green — the ground observer's perspective: figure, velocity arrow, and parabola (ground frame S)

Step 3: Experiment with Velocity

Move the slider and drop again. Higher V → wider parabola on the right, while the left panel always shows the same straight fall. The vertical motion — and the fall time — are identical in both frames.

The Galilean Transformation Equations

The two views are connected by:

  • x(t) = x′(t) + V·t — ground position = train position + train displacement
  • y(t) = y′(t) — vertical position is the same in both frames
  • t = t′ — time is absolute (same for everyone in Galilean physics)

Since x′ = 0 (ball stays above the drop point in S′), the ground observer sees x = V·t and y = H − ½gt² — a parabola.

💡 Tip: Set V = 0 — both panels become identical, confirming the frames coincide when the train is stationary!

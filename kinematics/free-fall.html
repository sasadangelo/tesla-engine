🎮 How to Use the Simulation

Step 1: Choose a gravity preset

Select the gravitational environment using the "Gravity preset" combo box. Earth has g = 9.81 m/s², Mars 3.72 m/s², Jupiter 24.79 m/s². "Deep Space" sets g = 0: neither object falls — they float in weightlessness.

Step 2: Set air resistance

Drag the "Air resistance" slider to the left (Vacuum) or right (Dense air). At zero the medium offers no drag — this is the vacuum experiment that Galileo could only imagine. Increase it to see how air separates the heavy lead ball from the light feather.

💡 Key insight: Start with the slider at Vacuum and notice that both objects land at exactly the same time. Then increase air resistance and repeat — the feather slows dramatically while the lead ball barely changes. The difference is air, not weight.

Step 3: Drop!

Click "Drop!" to release both objects from rest at the same height (100 m). Watch the trails, velocity readouts, and landing times in the stats panel.

🧪 Suggested Experiments

Experiment 1 — Galileo's vacuum

Setup: Earth gravity, air resistance = 0

Expected result: Lead ball and feather land simultaneously. Landing time: t = √(2·100/9.81) ≈ 4.52 s

Experiment 2 — Earth with air

Setup: Earth gravity, air resistance ≈ 2.0

Expected result: Lead ball lands near 4.52 s; feather reaches terminal velocity and takes much longer.

Experiment 3 — Mars vs Earth (vacuum)

Setup: Switch between Earth and Mars with air resistance = 0

Expected result: Both objects still land together on Mars, but later (t ≈ 7.33 s). Gravity changes when they land, never who lands first.

Experiment 4 — Deep Space

Setup: Deep Space preset

Expected result: g = 0 — neither object falls. This shows that "falling" requires a gravitational field, not mass.
